03: Individual Labor Relations

Rights, Obligations, and the impact of Social Network Law

I. The Legal Framework of the Employee

The employee-employer relationship is governed by the Labor Code and Collective Agreements. Unlike a freelancer, an employee enters a "Protective Status."

1. Essential Rights (The Employer's Debt)

  • Remuneration: The right to a salary at least equal to the minimum wage (SMIC or Convention minimums).
  • Professional Training: The employer must ensure the employee can adapt to their job as technology changes.
  • Health and Safety: The "Obligation of Result." If an employee is injured or suffers a burnout, the employer is often held strictly liable.

2. Essential Obligations (The Employee's Debt)

  • Execution of Work: Performing the tasks defined in the job description with care.
  • Respect for Internal Rules: Following the "Règlement Intérieur" regarding discipline, safety, and digital use.
  • Obligation of Discretion: Not revealing company secrets or technical processes.

II. Social Network Law: The Modern Battlefield

As digital marketing students, this is your most important topic. The boundary between private life and professional life has disappeared.

Can an employee be fired for a "Like" or a "Post"?

The law uses the "Abuse of Freedom of Expression" criteria. An employee is free to speak, UNLESS their speech becomes:

  • Injurious: Direct insults to management or colleagues.
  • Defamatory: Spreading false information about the company's products.
  • Excessive: Using terms that create a toxic atmosphere.

Crucial Detail: If your social media profile is 100% private (only friends), it is harder for an employer to fire you. If it is public (Twitter/LinkedIn/Public Instagram), you are representing the company.
